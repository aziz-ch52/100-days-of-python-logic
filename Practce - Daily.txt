## Part 1: The Logic-Building Framework

Before you write a single line of Python, you must pass the **"Plain English Test."** If you cannot explain the solution to a 5-year-old in English, you have no business writing it in Python.

### The 4-Step Execution Method

1. **Input/Output Mapping:** Write down exactly what goes in and exactly what must come out.
2. **The Manual Solution:** Solve the problem on paper with a pen. If the task is "reverse a string," physically write how your brain does it. (e.g., "I look at the last letter, write it down, move left...")
3. **Pseudocode (The Logic Bridge):** Write the steps in plain English sentences.
4. **Python Translation:** Only now do you convert those sentences into Python syntax.

## Part 3: Python "Pro" Notes for You

To code better, you need to use Pythonic ways while maintaining your specific style.

* **Avoid "Magic Numbers":** Never use raw numbers in your logic. Use variables with clear names.
* **The Power of `range()`:** Master `range(start, stop, step)`. It is the heart of most Python logic.
* **DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself):** If you copy-paste code more than twice, put it in a function.
* **Use `enumerate()`:** Instead of manually tracking an index in a loop, use `for i, val in enumerate(list):`.
* **Your Personal Style Guide:** Since you prefer explicit parentheses, keep them consistent. It helps you see the "boundaries" of your logic.

> **Crucial Advice:** When you fail—and you will—do **not** Google the answer immediately. Stare at the problem for 20 minutes. Draw it on paper. The "growth" happens in the 20 minutes of struggle, not in the 2 seconds of reading a solution.
